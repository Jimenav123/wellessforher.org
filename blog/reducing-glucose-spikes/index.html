The Main Reason My PCOS Wasn’t Getting Better: Reducing Glucose Spike in 5 Steps

When I first started my PCOS healing journey, my primary focus was on regulating insulin. Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas, which acts as a "key," to facilitating the storage of excess glucose in muscle, fat, and liver cells.

But, what exactly is glucose?

Glucose, a type of sugar derived from the foods we consume, serves as an energy source. As it travels through the bloodstream to our cells, it is referred to as blood sugar.

The distribution of glucose to various cells, muscle, liver, or fat, depends on the body's energy requirements and the storage capacity of each tissue. So, when insulin is stored in fat tissue, it contributes to the accumulation of body fat. The connection between PCOS and glucose spikes is significant, as many women with PCOS experience insulin resistance. This insulin resistance implies that the body's cells become less responsive to efficiently absorb glucose from the bloodstream, leading to disruptions in the normal regulation of blood sugar levels.

Some symptoms that I personally experienced included:

  • Fatigue
  • Inflammation
  • Brain fog
  • Weight gain
  • Increased hunger
  • Headache

Here Is What I Started Doing to Reduce That Glucose Spike

Food Order

1. Eating my food in order. By eating your foods in the right order you can reduce the glucose spike by up to 75%. The order is veggies first, then proteins and fats, and lastly starches and sugars. This is beneficial because it's been shown to slow down the digestion process.

No Sweets for Breakfast

2. Eating sweet foods for breakfast can lead to a rapid increase in blood glucose levels, given that they are quickly broken down into glucose during digestion. So I make sure that I consume a high protein, savory breakfast to help me avoid my cravings. Ex: Spinach, eggs, then sourdough bread.

Apple Cider Vinegar

3. Taking a shot of apple cider vinegar diluted in a cup of water before eating a meal has benefited my digestion and has been shown to reduce the spike of glucose by at least 20%.

Movement After Meals

4. Moving after eating for at least 10 minutes. When you exercise it sends a signal to your body that your muscles are contracted which allows your muscles to take up more glucose from your bloodstream as fuel. Anything can work from; walking, dancing, calf raises if you're in the office, stretching, etc.

Low Glycemic Index

5. Last but not least, eating in a low glycemic index. This does not mean that I diet or never eat outside, but it sure has helped me deal with my energy, bloating, and brain fog. This happens because low GI foods cause a slower rise in blood sugars. You can find a chart of what is low glycemic here: https://www.verywellhealth.com/glycemic-index-chart-for-common-foods-1087476

Honestly, incorporating these practical tools into my routine has really impacted both my energy levels and inflammation. Over the past two years, I've consistently followed these practices even when it seems challenging or tiring. Personally, for me, the symptoms I have from my glucose spikes are far worse than the effort that is required for these four steps.

To make this process easier, I've adopted the habit of carrying nuts or seaweed crackers with me. This way, if I ever feel hungry during my busy day and want to avoid for example eating a naked carbohydrate, I pair it with these options to reduce my glucose spike. I felt compelled to share these tips that have worked for me in the hope that they might benefit you too. Making my PCOS symptoms completely reduce or disappear.
